Spot Price Retail Contracts - How to manage your energy price risk?

17 November 2018

Innovation in retail contracts

In recent years, deregulation and increasing competition in retail markets has been spurring a voluminous number of new entrant retailers into the market. The ever-growing focus on consumer value has begun steering us away from traditional FPVV contracts, towards newer and more innovative directions; new business models that attempt to disrupt the market by combatting costs down, providing greater benefits and value to end-consumers.


One development that is radically changing the way consumers think about and engage with the industry is the growing uptake of users on spot price contracts. These types of contracts have had a great deal of interest from participants, and also caused much public controversy. Regulators have recently begun exploring the best mechanisms and policies to govern such contracts. Given the uncertainty of the spot market, market participants are also skeptical of the net realisable value for their respective circumstances.

For some, these contracts have already helped reduce their overall energy costs - namely for larger-scale consumers who are able to independently manage their wholesale price risk and spot price exposure. For others however, the net value from such contracts haven't been as fruitful. This is particularly the case for smaller-scale consumers who find that hedging opportunities are somewhat limited without the appropriate infrastructure and prudential security available, especially when spot prices spike and remain high for prolonged periods at a time (i.e., such as during a dry year or month).

A more tailored set of strategies is therefore required by smaller consumers to manage this risk. A good understanding of both spot price dynamics and their own power consumption profiles will greatly help to identify any opportunities for smaller consumers to curtail their energy costs when faced with prolonged periods of high prices.


Reducing energy costs when spot prices spike

Some of the options that can be (somewhat easily) adopted by smaller consumers, to either hedge or mitigate their overall energy consumption levels during times of high spot prices are:

  1. Hedging their exposure to high spot prices by entering into financial contracts (e.g., CfDs, ASX futures contracts, etc.).
  2. Switching to alternative sources of generation (e.g., rooftop solar), or investing in backup generation.
  3. Taking advantage of battery storage technologies.
  4. Negotiating a fixed-price contract to mitigate part, or all of their spot price risk.
  5. Reducing load and consumption levels at real-time, or within the same day.

Engagement in the above measures also opens up further opportunities for the incumbent consumer. For example, investing in smart technologies and automation systems to curtail short-term energy consumption means that the consumer can also participate in an accreditated demand response programme and generate further income from their invested energy technologies.


All together, now

Before considering the above, it is important for all participants to first understand and review the best option, or combination of options, available to them given their particular circumstances and available resources. Not all load profiles are the same, and not all technical specifications for equipment is similar. Undertaking a stocktake of technologies, and a review of expected consumption patterns, load generation, operating costs and budgets available, will help inform the optimal measures to take to curtail price risk based on the user's risk appetite.

More accurate information about their own day-to-day operations and energy use, and a deeper understanding of energy market dynamics also enables consumers to be able to better thrive and engage in the energy markets, empowering consumers to act and make better business decisions that can materially affect their bottom line profits and net cash flow.
